Worlds is the engine for personalized, alignment-driven software. By providing a
stateful substrate for autonomous agents, it enables the practical application
of Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback (RLHF) to steer high-dimensional
model behavior.

## Probability reshaping

In its most raw form, RLHF is the process of sculpting a probability landscape.
An LLM starts as a map of all possible word sequences. RLHF acts as a series of
nudges in token space:

- Logit shift: increases raw scores for preferred paths and suppresses undesired
  ones.
- Softmax filter: ensures that during inference, the model is statistically
  driven toward helpfulness and safety, effectively blocking parts of the token
  maze that lead to poor outcomes.

---

## Engineering constraints

In practice, this is a balancing act managed by the KL Divergence penalty. This
mathematical anchor prevents the model from drifting too far from its original
language capabilities.

### Reward hacking

Without this constraint, the model suffers from reward hacking, where it finds
cheats in the token space—like repetitive or sycophantic phrasing—to maximize
the reward score while losing actual utility. Worlds provides the verifiable
context required to monitor and mitigate these hacks by grounding model outputs
in facts.

## Autonomous trajectories

When applied to continual learning autonomous systems, the tokens evolve into
actions and the sentences become trajectories.

- World models: RLHF aligns the system’s internal simulation of cause-and-effect
  with human values.
- Recursive learning: The system uses feedback to update its policy in
  real-time, navigating the tension between learning new environmental peaks and
  avoiding the catastrophic forgetting of previous safety protocols.

---

## Recursive quality loop

The future of intentional agency relies on the recursive quality loop. This is
where the recursive optimization becomes a core feature rather than a bug.

### Recursive state refinement

Traditional databases rely on static schemas, which act as a lossy filter for
human preference. In Worlds, human feedback is ingested as verifiable triples in
the world itself (e.g., `query:xyz :hasFeedback :positive`).

This creates a self-refining memory system:

1.  Agent performs query $\rightarrow$ Human gives feedback
2.  Feedback becomes a triple in the ledger
3.  Ontology evolution: The agent optimizes its own representation of data
    (schema) to align with the refined feedback.
4.  Reshaped retrieval: Future queries follow the reshaped probability landscape
    toward high-reward paths.

### Academic grounding

The idea that recursive oversight is necessary for true alignment is supported
by major AI research:

- Recursive reward modeling (RRM): Jan Leike et al. (OpenAI) argue in
  ["Scalable agent alignment via reward modeling"](https://arxiv.org/abs/1811.07871)
  that breaking complex evaluations into recursive sub-tasks is the only way to
  scale oversight and align models with nuanced human intent.
- Recursive self-improvement (RSI): The theoretical basis for systems found in
  the work of Nick Bostrom and Eliezer Yudkowsky, where meta-optimization of
  internal schemas leads to optimal state configurations.

### Bottom line

RLHF transforms a model that can predict anything into a system that prefers
preferred outcomes. You can use preference data to steer high-dimensional
behavior through a verifiable, recursive loop of recorded state refinement.
