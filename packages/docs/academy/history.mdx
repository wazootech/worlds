---
title: "History of the Worlds ecosystem"
description:
  "Tracing the evolution of communication and computing from early language to
  the global item store."
---

The concepts driving the **Worlds ecosystem**—such as universal data
portability, item graphs, and transclusion—build upon nearly a century of
foundational computing research and visionary experiments.

### The early history of communication

The story of human coordination begins with the externalization of thought. Our
first great technology was **symbolic representation**—the moment early humans
began drawing images on cave walls to symbolize things in the world. This
allowed us to share survival tactics and "climb to one another" through shared
meaning.

As civilization advanced, we moved from these pictorial symbols to the
**Phoenician alphabet**, inventing a way to freeze language itself in physical
space. For the first time, a thought could outlive the thinker. This mechanical
recording reached its analog climax with the **Gutenberg press**. Knowledge was
no longer trapped in fragile, isolated manuscripts; the mechanization of
information meant ideas could finally "travel as fast as travel," democratizing
the human mind.

### Ancient invisible data: The khipu

Beyond writing, humans pioneered sophisticated, non-alphabetic ways to interface
with data. The **khipu** (or quipu) was an Andean system of knotted strings that
recorded information through a complex "binary" of knot types, colors, and
tactile textures. Used for both numeric record-keeping and narrative histories,
khipus represent a profound loss of specialized knowledge—the keys to "reading"
these multidimensional item graphs were largely destroyed during the Spanish
conquest, leaving their narratives lost to time.

### 1945: The memex

Post-WWII, Vannevar Bush proposed the **memex** in his essay _"As We May
Think."_ He envisioned a mechanical desk that could interlink separate texts and
records, mirroring the associative nature of the human mind rather than the
rigid hierarchies of libraries.

### 1960s: Hypertext and augmenting intellect

Douglas Engelbart’s 1962 framework, _"Augmenting Human Intellect,"_ argued that
computers should expand human capability rather than just simulate physical
methods. At the 1968 "Mother of All Demos," he demonstrated **hypertext** and
the concept of **transclusion**—the ability to embed a live view of one document
into another, reaching beyond the limits of static files.

### 1980s: The computer as a medium and the desktop detour

Alan Kay realized the personal computer was a "medium" capable of changing
civilization's thought patterns. However, to make computers understandable to
the masses, the industry adopted the **desktop metaphor**. We placed our digital
minds into "filing cabinets" (folders and apps), which inadvertently severed the
natural links between related pieces of information.

### 1990s: The global graph and compound documents

Tim Berners-Lee’s World Wide Web successfully turned humanity's public
information into a densely linked graph. Simultaneously, systems like
**HyperCard** and **OpenDoc** explored deep integration and portable data,
treating software as a collection of reusable components rather than isolated
silos.

### 2000s to present: The Worlds ecosystem

Despite the "app" retreat, niche communities kept the vision of a linked
ecosystem alive through personal wikis and **notes graphs** like Obsidian.
Today, the **Worlds ecosystem** finally shatters the app icon. By treating data
as a universal "item store" rather than isolated files, it brings the universal
linking of the Web and the malleability of OpenDoc to the very core of our
digital environments.

Worlds allows humans to operate in perfect symbiosis with their digital
environments—where every piece of information is a portal, and every connection
is a path to new discovery.
